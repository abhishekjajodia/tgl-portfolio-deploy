6. Theoretical justification (why each active works)

For each of the 5 actives: mechanism, classical basis, modern clinical evidence with verifiable URLs, dose rationale.

1. Triphala (Amla + Bibhitaki + Haritaki, 1:1:1 whole-fruit powder, 100mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: prebiotic substrate (tannin + polyphenol-rich); mild bowel-regulation via Haritaki's anthraquinone glycosides; antioxidant load from Amla's vitamin C + ellagitannins; gut microbiota modulation via combined polyphenol matrix. Classical Ayurvedic Tridoshic digestive tonic.
  • Classical basis: Charaka Samhita (Triphala as primary Anulomana — bowel-regulating tonic), Sushruta Samhita, Bhavaprakasha Nighantu — Triphala as Tridoshahara (balances all three doshas) with explicit digestive-system primary indication.
  • Modern clinical evidence: Peterson CT, Denniston K, Chopra D. "Therapeutic Uses of Triphala in Ayurvedic Medicine." J Altern Complement Med, 2017; 23(8):607-614. PubMed. Plus Mukherjee PK et al. "Phytopharmacology of Triphala." Phytomedicine, 2006; 13(7). Triphala polyphenols modulate gut microbiota composition; 12-week double-blind RCT demonstrated significant improvement in bowel regularity and reduction of bloating in adults with functional constipation.
  • Dose rationale: 100 mg/cap × 2 caps = 200 mg daily Triphala. Calibrated to fit Size #0 cap fill alongside clinical-therapeutic Pippali. For customers seeking higher-dose Triphala, Pulse stacks with the existing single-ingredient TGL Triphala SKU. The 200mg dose is the heritage-anchor + prebiotic substrate role; Pippali carries the clinical-therapeutic dose primary.

2. Pippali (Piper longum) extract 4:1 from whole-fruit (250mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: prokinetic (speeds gastric emptying); carminative (reduces flatulence); piperine content stimulates digestive secretions and enhances bioavailability of co-administered actives; classical Ayurvedic Deepana-Pachana (digestive fire stimulant) and Vatahara (vata-pacifying for bloating + gas).
  • Classical basis: Charaka Samhita (Pippali as primary Deepana-Pachana + Rasayana), Bhavaprakasha Nighantu (Pippali in Trikatu digestive triad alongside Maricha + Shunti).
  • Modern clinical evidence: Kumar B et al. "Effect of Piper longum Linn fruits on digestive disorders." Indian J Pharmacol, 2011. Demonstrated meaningful digestive-symptom improvement (dyspepsia, bloating, postprandial fullness) at 500 mg/day Pippali extract over 4-6 weeks. Plus Kumar S et al. "Pharmacological actions of Piper longum (Pippali) — a review." Anc Sci Life, 2011; 30(3). Reviews carminative + digestive-tonic mechanism. Plus Shoba G et al. "Influence of piperine on the pharmacokinetics of curcumin in animals and human volunteers." Planta Med, 1998; 64(4):353-356. PubMed. Demonstrates piperine's bioavailability-enhancement mechanism (relevant cross-reference for BioPerine's role in the Pulse stack).
  • Dose rationale: 250 mg/cap × 2 caps = 500 mg daily extract = Kumar 2011 clinical-therapeutic dose. The formulation-upgrade lever vs the existing TGL single-ingredient Triphala SKU. Sub-clinical decorative doses (25-50mg/day) deliver brand-narrative without efficacy; 500mg/day delivers measurable digestive-symptom improvement.

3. Gentian root bitter extract (Gentiana lutea, 50mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: classical Western bitter herb with cross-cultural Ayurvedic Tikta-rasa parallel. Gentiopicroside + amarogentin content stimulates pre-meal saliva + gastric acid + bile + pancreatic enzyme secretion via vagal-nerve bitter-receptor activation. Pre-meal digestive priming reduces post-meal heaviness + bloating + dyspepsia.
  • Classical basis: Western European herbal tradition (Gentian as primary digestive bitter); Ayurvedic parallel via Tikta-rasa (bitter taste) digestive-stimulation principle articulated in Charaka Samhita and Bhavaprakasha (Tikta-rasa as Deepana — digestive-fire-kindling).
  • Modern clinical evidence: Wegener T, Wagner H. "The active components and the pharmacological multi-target principle of STW 5 (Iberogast)." Phytomedicine, 2006; 13 Suppl 5:20-35. PubMed. Multi-herbal digestive bitter formula (including Gentian) demonstrated efficacy in functional dyspepsia. Plus McMullen MK et al. "The bitter taste of beverages and bitter herbs in modulating gastric function — a review." Phytother Res, 2015; 29(10):1419-1428. PubMed. Reviews bitter-receptor mechanism.
