1. Name + Elevator Pitch

Name: Cycle (working name; final lock pending TM clearance per TGL-P1-DEC-007).

Elevator pitch (3 sentences, repeatable by anyone on the team):

Cycle is TGL's flagship women's wellness SKU, designed for the 28-35 year old Indian woman who suffers PMS and period symptoms 1-2 days every month and is tired of relying on painkillers, birth control pills, or bitter-tasting Ayurvedic kashayams. Two plant-cellulose capsules a day during her cycle window deliver six classical Ayurvedic herbs (Shatavari, Ashoka, Lodhra, Manjistha, Moringa, Black Sesame) to ease cramps, regulate her cycle, and reduce PMS bloating over 3 cycles of consistent use. It's the SKU our existing 10K+ TGL customers were already pulling moringa for; Cycle gives that latent demand the right formulation.

2. Why we're building Cycle (motivation)

Four reasons, in priority order:

1. Existing demand we're under-serving. TGL's 10K+ existing customers already pull our moringa capsules for "period regulation". It's the dominant theme in our Customer Reviews Repository. But moringa alone is a thin formulation for cycle care: it adds nutritive support without moving the cramp / flow / PMS levers. Cycle takes that latent demand and gives it the formulation it deserves. Moringa stays as the heritage signature carrying the brand thread; Shatavari, Ashoka, Lodhra, Manjistha, and Sesame do the actual work.

2. Pricing whitespace at ₹600-900 sale. Period-care has two visible tiers in India today:

  • Pharma-tier: Gynoveda Period Cure ₹1,200-1,800, doctor-coded, complex multi-cap kit, intimidating.
  • Mass-tier: Patanjali, Baidyanath at ₹150-300, low-trust, kashayam-format, bitter.

In between, a single accessible-premium player exists (Earthful HerSupport at ₹589, but PCOS-anchored, not PMS-anchored). Cycle slots cleanly into the premium D2C band where no PMS-specific SKU exists today.

3. Best portfolio fit across all 10 SKUs (TGL-Fit score 28/30, top of the portfolio). Cycle wins on four dimensions: customer-voice match (existing customers ask for it), founder authenticity (Deepika's audience is dominantly women 28-42), pillar alignment (all-Indian supply chain, no flagged imports, fully Ayurvedic stack), and acquisition friction (warm cohort, not cold launch).

4. Cleanest repurchase mechanic in the portfolio. Cyclic-use (15-21 days/month around her cycle, then pause, then return) creates a built-in monthly rhythm. Cycle doesn't have to convince her to "try a daily wellness habit"; it slots into the rhythm her body already has. That makes it the cleanest subscription play in the portfolio, with 6-week cadence aligned to consumption.

Why now, why paired with Bloom: Cycle alone is acquisition. Bloom (daily women's multi) is retention + cross-sell. The Q1 pair is designed so the same customer enters via Cycle for her acute symptom, gets warm-pitched into Bloom as her foundational daily routine, and starts the bundle-attach pattern that funds Phase 2 unlocks (Reset PCOS + Root Hair). One launches the brand; the other fuels the unit economics.

Single-sentence motivation: Cycle is the SKU TGL's existing customers were already asking for, sitting in the price band where no one else has built a credible PMS-specific Ayurvedic blend, designed to be the acquisition gateway into a bigger women's wellness portfolio.

She's an urban Indian woman, primarily South India (Tamil Nadu the strongest cluster, Karnataka + Andhra + Telangana close behind), expanding to metros nationally over time, household income ₹6-25L, multilingual (English-Tamil or English-Hindi mix is the dominant register; Deepika's natural speaking voice works in both registers), educated, working or running a household with kids 0-8. Regional concentration matters at launch. TGL's strongest existing footprint is in Tamil Nadu, with secondary strength across Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, and Telangana. Phase 1 launch indexes on this region first: heavier Meta targeting weighting, founder content with English-Tamil and English-Telugu language layering, regional creator partnerships, packaging copy that doesn't alienate non-Hindi-first audiences. The brand expands outward (Mumbai, Bangalore, Delhi metros) once the South Indian cohort is solid. This isn't a permanent regional cap; it's a launch-sequence choice that uses the existing trust footprint instead of fighting cold acquisition in markets where TGL has zero customer density.

She has painful periods (dysmenorrhea, 50-60% of menstruating Indian women), often irregular cycles (20-25%), sometimes heavy flow (15-20%), and PMS (mood, bloating, cramps) for 3-7 days every month. She's not pathologically diagnosed; she's symptomatic. 1-2 days every month, she leaves work early or struggles through it.

4. What the product does (the lived experience over 3-6 months)

Cycle is not a painkiller, and we don't market it as one. Painkillers shut down a cramp in 30 minutes; Cycle works on a different timescale. It's a body-rhythm intervention: gradual cycle regulation + reduced symptom intensity over 3+ cycles of consistent use. Setting that expectation honestly is what protects us from the "I took it for two weeks and felt nothing" review wall that kills D2C wellness brands.

Here's what she actually experiences if she takes 2 caps/day during her cyclic window for 6 months:

Cycle 1 (first 30 days): the compliance test.

Minimal perceived change. Maybe a small mood-lift in luteal week (Manjistha + Sesame). Maybe slightly less bloating. Often, nothing she'd register. This is the highest drop-off window in any wellness D2C; most customers quit here. The marketing job in Cycle 1 is not to sell efficacy; it's to sell patience. Deepika's content arc in launch month is "give it 3 cycles before you decide. Real plants take time to work."

Cycles 2-3 (60-90 days): the first signal.

A meaningful proportion of consistent users start to report:

  • Cramp intensity reduction (not elimination, but Day 1 stops feeling like a write-off day)
  • PMS bloating reduction (luteal week feels less heavy)
  • Mood swings less sharp
  • Sleep disturbance pre-period less acute

The user pattern at this stage: she finishes Pack 2, recognises she felt better last cycle than the one before, and decides "let me see if it sticks." This is the conversion moment from trial to loyal customer. Subscription retention math says: if she finishes Pack 2 and reorders Pack 3, she's typically a 12-month customer.

Cycles 4-6 (4-6 months): regularisation kicks in.

For users with mild-moderate cycle irregularity (the dominant subset of TGL's customer base), Cycle starts to settle her cycle window. She knows when her period will arrive within a 2-3 day envelope vs the 5-10 day envelope she lived with before. Cramp intensity continues to soften. Some users report cyclical acne (a Manjistha-sensitive marker) clearing. The "I forgot I had PMS this month" moment lands somewhere in this window.

6+ months: maintenance and step-down.

Once the body is regulated, some users step down from full cyclic-window dosing (15-21 days) to period-only dosing (5-7 days/cycle). Pack consumption stretches from 1.5 months to 2.5-3 months. Subscription cadence adjusts; annual revenue per customer drops modestly, but lifetime stays high. Others stay on full cyclic-window dose for the consistency. We don't push customers either way; the product trains its own dose.

What we're NOT promising (so customers and Deepika both stay credible):

Not this Because
Pain shutdown in 30 minutes Painkillers do that; this isn't one
"Cure" of dysmenorrhea / PCOD / endometriosis Disease-coded claims violate Schedule VI; also untrue
Hormonal therapy alternative She'll need her gynaec for diagnosed conditions
Replacement for dietary changes or sleep hygiene Cycle stacks with lifestyle, doesn't replace it
Effect inside 30 days Too aggressive; wrong expectation; review wall

The honest customer message Deepika repeats across content: "Built on Moringa. Powered by Ayurveda. Two caps a day during your cycle window. Give it 3 cycles before you decide. Real plants take time to work, that's what makes it work."

6. Theoretical justification (why each active works)

For each of the 6 actives: mechanism, classical basis, modern clinical evidence with verifiable URLs, dose rationale.

1. Shatavari extract (4-5% saponins, 200mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: phytoestrogenic steroidal saponins (shatavarins I-IV) bind weakly to estrogen receptors. Modulates estrogen activity (agonist where deficient, antagonist where excess). Adaptogenic action on the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian (HPO) axis. Mild diuretic helps PMS bloating.
  • Classical basis: Charaka Samhita, Sushruta Samhita, Bhavaprakasha Nighantu. Primary Stree-rasayana (women's tonic). Single most-cited herb in classical women's health pharmacology.
  • Modern clinical evidence: Pandey et al. "Impact of stress on female reproductive health disorders: Possible beneficial effects of shatavari (Asparagus racemosus)." Biomed Pharmacother, 2018; 103:46-49. PubMed. Plus Alok et al. "Plant profile, phytochemistry and pharmacology of Asparagus racemosus (Shatavari): A review." Asian Pacific Journal of Tropical Disease, 2013; 3(3):242-251. PMC full text.
  • Dose rationale: 200mg/cap × 2 caps = 400mg daily extract. Delivers 16-20mg shatavarins/day, in the classical therapeutic range (200-500mg extract daily for cycle regularity).

2. Ashoka extract (~40% tannins, 8:1 from whole-bark, 25mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: tannins + flavonoids + phytosterols. Uterine stimulant + tonic action. Reduces uterine contractility (cramp relief). Astringent action on excessive menstrual flow.
  • Classical basis: Bhavaprakasha + classical Ashokarishta fermented preparations (centuries-old gold-standard menstrual formulation). M.A. Iyengar (1934) classical pharmacology positions Ashoka as the primary uterine tonic.
  • Modern clinical evidence: Multiple small Indian RCTs (2010-2018) document Ashoka bark extract reducing dysmenorrhea pain intensity by 30-40% over 2-3 cycles. No single landmark paper at meta-analysis quality. Reference review: Saraca asoca pharmacology review (IRJMETS, 2023). Dominant evidence base remains classical-textual.
  • Dose rationale: 25mg extract/cap × 2 caps = 50mg daily extract = approximately 400mg whole-bark equivalent (classical dose range 200-500mg whole-bark daily).

3. Lodhra extract (~30% tannins, 8:1 from whole-bark, 13mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: astringent via tannins. Mild estrogenic-modulating action. Hemostatic.
  • Classical basis: Bhavaprakasha. Classical Sangrahi (astringent). Combined with Ashoka in Ashokarishta and adjacent classical formulations.
  • Modern clinical evidence: Limited modern RCTs. This is a classical-traditional-strong, modern-thin active. We use it because the classical pairing logic with Ashoka holds and the bioactive (tannin) is well-characterised. No specific landmark clinical paper to cite at this stage.
  • Dose rationale: 13mg extract/cap × 2 caps = 26mg daily = approximately 200mg whole-bark equivalent. Conservative classical dose; classical formulations use 250-500mg whole-bark for severe menorrhagia.

4. Manjistha extract (~10% anthraquinones, 8:1 from whole-root, 13mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: anthraquinones + glycosides. Classical Raktashodhaka (blood purifier). Modern: anti-inflammatory + lymphatic decongestant. Addresses cyclical skin clarity.
  • Classical basis: Charaka Samhita primary Raktaprasadana herb. Bhavaprakasha + Sushruta both feature it.
  • Modern clinical evidence: anti-inflammatory in vitro studies; cycle-specific clinical data limited. Traditional-strong, modern-emerging. No specific landmark clinical paper to cite at this stage.
  • Dose rationale: 13mg extract/cap × 2 caps = 26mg daily = approximately 200mg whole-root equivalent (modern Ayurvedic supplement dose typically 100-300mg whole-root).

5. Moringa whole-leaf powder (heat-treated, TGL captive supply, 75mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: whole-plant nutritive (iron, calcium, vitamin C, antioxidants, chlorophyll). Not a primary cycle-regulation active; supports replenishment of period-related nutrient losses, especially iron.
  • Folk + modern basis: established nutraceutical literature on moringa as iron + folate + B-complex source. Iron deficiency exacerbates PMS symptoms; many menstruating Indian women are sub-clinically deficient.
  • Modern evidence: Alegbeleye, O.O. "How Functional Is Moringa oleifera? A Review of Its Nutritive, Medicinal, and Socioeconomic Potential." Food Nutr Bull, 2018. PubMed. Plus 2023 systematic review: "The Impact of Moringa oleifera Supplementation on Anemia and other Variables during Pregnancy and Breastfeeding: A Narrative Review." PMC full text. Strong nutritive evidence base; not specifically PMS-clinical, but population-nutritional support is well-established.
  • Dose rationale: 75mg/cap × 2 caps = 150mg daily. Calibrated heritage signature dose (Tier A 50-75mg band per Moringa Foundation principle). Substantive enough to carry the moringa thread; calibrated to avoid stacking-overload when taken alongside other moringa-bearing TGL SKUs.
  • Brand role: heritage signature carrying the "every cap a piece of our farms" cross-portfolio narrative. Works in dual mode as nutritive + brand thread.

6. Black sesame extract (40-50% sesamin, hexane-free, 50mg/cap)

  • Mechanism: sesamin lignans + phytoestrogens (sesamolin). Calcium-rich. Classical Ayurvedic Vata-pacifier in lower abdomen, reduces cramping.
  • Classical basis: Charaka Samhita. Tila in gynaecological formulations. Used in classical Vata-shaman therapies for menstrual care.
  • Modern clinical evidence: Wu WH et al. "Sesame Ingestion Affects Sex Hormones, Antioxidant Status, and Blood Lipids in Postmenopausal Women." The Journal of Nutrition, 2006; 136(5):1270-1275. Oxford Academic. Confirmed sesame ingestion shifts endogenous estrogen markers; not period-specific RCTs but mechanism-aligned.
  • Dose rationale: 50mg extract/cap × 2 caps = 100mg daily extract. At 40-50% sesamin = 40-50mg sesamin/day, within bioactive range cited in lignan literature.

7. How we arrived at this product (the decision narrative)

Three layers of decisions that produced Cycle as the SKU it is.

Layer 1: Why Cycle as Phase 1 launch (not Reset, Bloom-alone, or Crest)

We evaluated all 10 SKUs across four cross-validating dimensions: (1) Volume potential (category size in India), (2) Profitability (margin × LTV potential), (3) TGL-fit (existing customer voice + pillar alignment), (4) Founder authenticity (Deepika's voice fits the segment). Cycle scored 28/30 on TGL-fit and ranked top across the four-lens triangulation (full rationale in 03-segment-prioritization.md). Four reasons it won:

  • Highest customer-voice match. Period regulation is the dominant theme in TGL's existing 10K+ customer review base. We are serving demand that is already there, not creating new demand.
  • Lowest acquisition friction. TGL's existing buyers convert directly. Reset (PCOS) requires a colder cohort; Crest (Menopause) needs creator partnerships TGL doesn't have yet; Root (Hair) is a high-CAC competitive category dominated by Be Bodywise.
  • Cleanest pricing whitespace. ₹600-900 PMS-anchored Ayurveda doesn't exist today. Earthful is PCOS-anchored; Gynoveda is pharma-tier ₹1,200+; Patanjali is ₹150 mass-tier. Direct path to category leadership at the accessible-premium tier.
  • All-Indian supply chain, fully Ayurvedic stack. No imports flagged. No D-Inositol natural-source uncertainty (that's SKU 2). No regulatory edge cases. Lowest execution risk for a Phase 1 launch.

Bloom (Daily Multi) joins Cycle in Q1 not because it's the second-best Phase 1 candidate in isolation, but because the Cycle + Bloom pair completes a unit-economics arc (Cycle = acquisition; Bloom = retention + cross-sell). Reset, Root, Crest, Calm, Nourish, Pulse, Glide, Spark all ship across Phases 2-6 once Cycle's Month 3 validation gate clears.

Lodhra INCLUDED Classical pair-with-Ashoka for heavy-flow patients. Doubles down astringent action.

Layer 3: Why this specific dose architecture (and not another)

Every dose was triangulated across three constraints:

  1. Classical therapeutic range (what Bhavaprakasha or modern Ayurvedic supplements actually use)
  2. Capsule fill feasibility (Size #0 = ~500mg max practical fill)
  3. 2-cap-per-day compliance ceiling (anything beyond 2 caps creates retention friction)

That triangulation forced the extract-first decision (Operational Principle 6, locked as TGL-P1-DEC-011). With 5 extracts + 1 whole-leaf base at 8:1 concentration on the bark/root extracts, we deliver classical-equivalent doses (Ashoka 400mg whole-bark equivalent, etc.) while staying inside the 375mg fill × 2 caps/day envelope.

Trade-offs we accepted, openly stated:

  • Some classical whole-plant phytocomplex synergy in Ashoka, Lodhra, Manjistha. Switching to standardised extracts loses some of the natural ratio of co-occurring compounds. Mitigated by specifying full-spectrum / spray-dried extracts (Akay, Plant Lipids both offer this format) rather than isolated-marker extracts.
  • Less supplier diversity for less-traded extracts (Ashoka, Lodhra extracts are smaller markets than the powders). Mitigated by multi-sourcing primary + backup per active.
  • Per-active mg numbers look small on label (Manjistha 13mg/cap reads weak to a customer doing math). Mitigated by labelling whole-plant equivalence on-pack ("Ashoka extract 25mg, equivalent to 200mg whole-bark").
  • Lodhra and Manjistha modern clinical evidence is thin. We use them on classical-textual basis, not modern-RCT evidence. Marketing language must respect this gap (no "clinically proven" claim language for these two specifically).

The decision trail in one sentence: Cycle is the SKU TGL's existing customer base voted for with their reviews; the 6-active stack is the smallest credible Ayurvedic women's-tonic blend that respects classical therapeutic ranges; the extract-first architecture is what made it fit a 2-cap-per-day Size #0 capsule without compromising clinical-equivalent dosing.

2. It respects her time and her body. 2 caps a day during her cyclic window. No 4-6 cap pharma protocol, no daily-forever multivitamin habit, no bitter kashayam she would avoid by dinner. Her body already has a monthly rhythm; Cycle slots into it instead of fighting it.

3. It tells her the truth about the timeline. "Give it 3 cycles" is the message. Not "results in 7 days." Not "instant relief." Real plants take time to work, and the brand says so directly. By Cycle 2, she starts to notice. By Cycle 4-6, the regularity settles. Honest expectation-setting builds the trust that compounds into a long subscription relationship.

4. It's built on something she can see. Deepika's face is on the brand. Deepika's farm grew the moringa in every cap. There is an ingredient list she can read, a sourcing story she can verify, a founder voice she trusts more than any influencer's. The brand is a person, not a marketing budget.

5. The price feels right for what she gets. ₹899 sale, ₹764 subscription, ~₹15 a day during her cyclic window. A quarter of what Gynoveda costs per day. A third of what Plix costs per day. Above Patanjali's ₹150 because Patanjali isn't what she would actually take. The price says "premium that is earned," not "premium because we said so."

Two things that do not show up in the matrix but matter in real life:

  • Deepika speaks her register. English-Tamil and English-Hindi natural mix. Most premium D2C wellness brands speak only English-first Mumbai-Bangalore-coded. Cycle's customer is South Indian-leaning, multilingual, and Deepika sounds like her aunt or her older cousin who happens to know about Ayurvedic herbs. The standardisation percentages on the label (4-5% saponins, 40% tannins, 40-50% sesamin) match what Deepika quotes in her content. Internal-external consistency builds trust.

The single-sentence right to win: Cycle wins because it speaks the customer's actual problem, fits her actual schedule, tells the truth about results, comes from a person she can see, and costs the right amount for the trust it asks of her.

12.1 How we judge vendor quality (the compliance framework)

When TGL evaluates an extract supplier, we score against three tiers. Tier 1 is mandatory (deal-breaker if missing). Tier 2 is strongly preferred (premium signal). Tier 3 is application-specific.

Tier 1: Mandatory (deal-breaker if missing)

Criterion Why it matters What we ask vendor to provide
FSSAI Central License Mandatory for any nutraceutical-grade extract sold in India License number + expiry date
GMP certification (WHO-GMP preferred, AYUSH-GMP minimum) Manufacturing process discipline Certificate copy + valid through date
Per-batch CoA (Certificate of Analysis) Verifies every shipment matches spec 3 recent CoAs from different batches
NABL-accredited lab inputs Independent QC verification Lab partner name + NABL accreditation number
HPLC or HPTLC standardisation method Reproducibility verification Method document + marker compound name
Pillar 1 extraction method Natural-source-only pillar compliance Confirmation: water / hydroalcoholic / CO2 / supercritical only. No synthetic-residue solvents. Hexane-free for sesame specifically.

Tier 2: Strongly preferred (premium signal)

Criterion Why it matters
ISO 22000 (food safety management) Premium production discipline
HACCP (food safety hazard analysis) Risk-based food safety
ISO 9001 (quality management) Process consistency
USDA Organic certification Where on-pack claim applies
Multi-batch reproducibility (3+ batches at same spec) Real consistency, not first-batch luck
24-month stability testing Shelf-life claim substantiation
Public CoA transparency (publishes lab reports) Trust signal benchmark (Earthful does this)
Audit-readiness (accepts third-party facility audit) Long-term partnership viability

Tier 3: Application-specific

Criterion When it applies
DNA barcoding capability MANDATORY for endangered species (Saraca asoca / Symplocos racemosa). Verify they can authenticate species per batch.
USP-NF or IP pharmacopeial alignment Where extract is referenced in official pharmacopeia
Vegan / vegetarian certification If on-pack claim relevant
Halal / Kosher If export market relevant
Allergen control protocol If facility processes nuts / dairy / gluten and we want allergen-free claim

10 questions to ask on every first vendor call:

  1. Show us 3 recent CoAs from different batches. Are markers within ±10% spec?
  2. What is your standardisation method (HPLC / HPTLC / UV-Vis)?
  3. What is your extraction process? Confirm water / hydroalcoholic / CO2 only.
  4. What is your typical lead time at our MOQ tier?
  5. What is your minimum order quantity (in kg)?
  6. What is your price per kg at our MOQ?
  7. Do you provide 24-month accelerated stability data?
  8. Will you accept TGL doing third-party verification testing on every batch (NABL lab)?
  9. Can you supply at scale if we grow 3-5x in 18 months?
  10. Have you supplied to other Indian D2C wellness brands? Names if shareable.

12.7 Regulatory feasibility

All 6 actives FSSAI-permitted under Schedule VI of the Health Supplements Regulations 2022. Ashoka + Lodhra warrant a regulatory consultant pre-formulation review at Week 4 (Bucket 6 ₹5-10L) because their primary classical use is medicinal (uterine tonic) and FSSAI scrutinises claim drift between nutraceutical and AYUSH-medicinal categories. Likely consultant outcome:

  • Permitted claim language: "supports menstrual comfort," "helps maintain regular menstrual cycles," "traditionally used in Ayurveda for women's wellness," "supports the body during the monthly cycle."
  • NOT permitted: "cures dysmenorrhea / PCOD / endometriosis," "treats period pain," "eliminates cramps," "hormonal therapy alternative."

Plus standard label compliance: "Not for medicinal use," "Consult your physician before use if pregnant, breastfeeding, or on medication," FSSAI license number, veg symbol (green dot), 24-month shelf life with stability substantiation, full per-active disclosure in mg.
